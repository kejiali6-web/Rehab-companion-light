Rehab Companion Light

Empowering stroke survivors with assistive devices that respond to effort — not just motion.

by Kejia Liu

Story / Problem & Motivation

My grandfather — once strong and independent — lost sensation on the right side of his body and his ability to speak after a diabetic stroke. During his recovery, he fought not only to regain control of his body, but to reclaim his dignity.

“More than losing control of my body, I fear losing the dignity and independence that make me human.”

Through caring for him, I witnessed how rehabilitation challenges go beyond physical limits — they test a person’s patience, pride, and hope.

Problem Observation Diary

  • Rehabilitation is slow and fragile — small setbacks can cause patients to lose motivation.
  • Caregivers struggle to balance assistance with patient privacy during toileting or dressing.
  • Loss of speech limits communication, leading to emotional isolation and unmet needs.
  • Reduced mobility leads to social withdrawal, which worsens long-term recovery.

Research & Inspiration

I have been exploring literature and fieldwork to understand the physiological and emotional dimensions of diabetic stroke recovery. My readings include:

Zubkovs, Vitalijs, et al. “Bioengineering a Glucose Oxidase Nanosensor for Near-Infrared Continuous Glucose Monitoring.” Nanoscale Advances, 2022.

Feldman, Eva L., et al. “Diabetic Neuropathy.” Nature Reviews Disease Primers, vol. 5, no. 1, 41, 2019. DOI link.

Kim, Sehee, et al. “Dysregulated Hypothalamic–Pituitary–Adrenal Axis Is Associated With Increased Inflammation and Worse Outcomes After Ischemic Stroke in Diabetic Mice.” Frontiers in Immunology, vol. 13, 864858, 2022. DOI link.

These studies deepen my understanding of how diabetes alters neural pathways and recovery responses — crucial for designing more adaptive, sensor-integrated rehabilitation tools.

Visiting Huiyang Medical Equipment Factory

During my field research, I visited Huiyang Medical Equipment Co., Ltd., a rehabilitation device manufacturer producing electric and manual wheelchairs, rollators, and shower chairs. Observing their production lines — from aluminum alloy frames to ergonomic mesh seating — gave me insight into how engineering design translates into human comfort and accessibility.

Huiyang’s designs emphasize mechanical safety, stability, and lightweight structure. This inspired me to consider integrating soft robotics and sensor feedback for monitoring patient posture, grip strength, and fatigue in real-time, improving user autonomy and confidence.

Source: Huiyang Medical Equipment Co., Ltd., 2025 Product Catalog.

What I’m Exploring

Core Problem

Current rehabilitation tools often lack human-centered design — focusing on movement, not motivation.

Technical Frontier

Exploring glucose nanosensors, wearable robotics, and adaptive feedback systems for stroke patients.

Market & Empathy

From factory visits to patient interviews — combining design, empathy, and engineering.

What I’m Building

My goal is to design assistive medical tools that blend empathy and engineering — empowering patients to regain independence, one small motion at a time.

This site is the beginning of my public build log, where I document how compassion becomes engineering — from idea sketches to physical prototypes.
