Project Overview

Rehab Companion Light started from my grandfather’s rehabilitation experience. Many of his hand-grip exercises were repetitive and lonely, with very little feedback or encouragement. I wanted to build a small device that can sense grip strength and respond with light and personalized audio feedback.

Week 1 – Story, problem, and background research

  • Recorded observations from my grandfather’s stroke rehabilitation and daily grip exercises.
  • Wrote the first version of the problem statement and basic constraints (low cost, portable, simple interface).
  • Reviewed papers and online resources on FSR-based grip sensing and stroke recovery training.

Week 2 – Talking to mentors & defining the first architecture

  • Discussed the idea with engineering faculty and received feedback on feasibility and scope.
  • Sketched a simple system architecture: FSR → Arduino → LED / buzzer feedback.
  • Created an initial list of required parts and drafted a modular design plan.

Week 3 – First working breadboard circuit

  • Built a breadboard circuit using an FSR-402, resistors, LEDs, and a buzzer.
  • Wrote basic Arduino code with a fixed pressure threshold to turn on LEDs and trigger the buzzer.
  • Tested different grip strengths and confirmed that the sensor readings changed reliably.

Week 4 – Connecting with the lab

  • Met with Dr. Gulick to review the technical approach and clarify clinical relevance.
  • Discussed potential risks (sensor durability, calibration, comfort) and got suggestions for next steps.
  • Defined short-term milestones: stable sensing, encouragement mechanism, and a safe grip structure.

Week 5 – Team formation and adaptive feedback logic

  • Formed a small cross-disciplinary team (Bioengineering + Electrical Engineering) to develop the prototype together.
  • Designed the logic for an adaptive threshold that can increase after successful repetitions.
  • Planned how to integrate a voice-recording module to replace the simple buzzer tone.

Week 6 – Potentiometer and first Fusion 360 ring model

  • Added a potentiometer so the difficulty can be adjusted by turning a knob.
  • Started modeling the lower-half grip ring in Fusion 360 to hold and protect the FSR.
  • Iterated on basic dimensions to balance comfort, sensor protection, and printability.

Week 7 – Audio feedback design and print preparation

  • Integrated a voice-recording playback module to deliver short, personalized encouragement messages.
  • Finalized the main box CAD that will hold the Arduino, power supply, and audio module.
  • Set initial 3D printing parameters and selected materials for the first physical prototype.

Week 8 – First 3D print and failure analysis

  • Printed the first grip ring and housing components using PLA.
  • Tested the PLA ring in hand and realized it was too rigid and uncomfortable for repeated squeezing.
  • Decided to switch to TPU (flexible filament) and adjust wall thickness in the next iteration.

Next steps

  • Re-print the grip ring with TPU and compare comfort with PLA.
  • Refine ring ergonomics for different hand sizes and grip styles.
  • Tune audio encouragement patterns (content, timing, and frequency).
  • Run repeated-use tests to evaluate sensor stability and drift.
  • Plan early user feedback sessions with volunteers.
